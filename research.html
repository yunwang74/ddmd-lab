Research

Methods, mechanisms, and materials design

We combine statistical mechanics, quantum chemistry, molecular simulations, and data science to build predictive models for functional materials.

Our work focuses on how structure, composition, active sites, and operating conditions govern electronic, chemical, mechanical, and thermal properties. The goal is to turn that mechanistic understanding into design rules for better catalysts, electrochemical devices, sensing platforms, and energy materials.

A defining feature of the group is the link between method development and application-driven discovery. We develop and apply advanced computational approaches to materials problems where realistic interfaces, surfaces, and multiscale effects matter.

Current emphasis: grand-canonical DFT, surface and interface chemistry, active-site analysis, and predictive design of functional materials.

Method Development and Computational Innovation

We develop and apply advanced multiscale computational methodologies, including grand-canonical density functional theory, statistical mechanics, and atomistic simulation, to study complex materials under realistic operating conditions.

  • Bridge scales: connect atomic structure, interfaces, and macroscopic performance.
  • Model realistically: capture surfaces, reactions, solvation, and operating environments.

Mechanistic Understanding of Materials and Interfaces

A major focus is uncovering how composition, defects, active sites, and interfacial structure govern reactivity, electronic structure, mechanical response, and thermal transport.

  • Active sites: identify and characterise the atomic features that control performance.
  • Structure-property links: explain why materials behave as they do under operating conditions.

Rational Design and Optimisation

The mechanistic picture is translated into design principles for catalysts, electrochemical devices, sensing materials, and advanced functional materials with better activity, selectivity, stability, and durability.

  • Design rules: use theory and data to guide composition and structure selection.
  • Application focus: energy conversion, energy storage, sensing, and environmental remediation.
